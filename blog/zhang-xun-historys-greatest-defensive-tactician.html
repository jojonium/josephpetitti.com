Zhang Xun: History's Greatest Defensive Tactician

Zhang Xun was a Chinese general during the Tang Dynasty, and one of my favorite historical figures. He used brilliant tactics to successfully defend two fortresses against overwhelming enemy strength, displaying extreme loyalty to his empire and protecting southern China from being conquered by Yan rebels practically single-handedly. Although he resorted to some unfortunate strategies, such as condoning mass cannibalism, Zhang is still a fascinating figure with a life story that reads like a movie script.

Background

The Tang Dynasty was a powerful empire that ruled China from 618 to 907, often regarded as a golden age in Chinese history. The first half of the dynasty's reign was a period of stability and relative peace. Unfortunately, this stability ended in 755 when a Tang general named An Lushan declared himself emperor of northern China, establishing a rival dynasty known as Yan.

At first the Yan made quick gains in conquering China. An Lushan treated captured Tang generals and officials well, leading more and more of them to rebel and join his side. They advanced south and captured the eastern Tang capital, Luoyang.

Zhang Xun's superior, Yang Wanshi, defected to the rebels' side. He tried to force Zhang to become his secretary general, but Zhang refused, instead swearing his loyalty to the Tang empire and took 1,000 loyal men to Yongqiu to defend against the Yan.

The Battle of Yongqiu

Yongqiu had been commanded by an official named Linghu Chao, who had also defected to the Yan side and become a general. He left the fortress on a campaign but returned to find it occupied by Zhang Xun and his Tang loyalists.

Yongqiu was an important stronghold because it acted as a gateway to the rich and under-defended southern regions of China. If the Tang could hold onto the area An Lushan would be confined in northern China, at least for a little while.

Linghu gathered an army of 15,000 rebels and attacked Yongqiu, hoping to recapture the fortress and open the way to southern China. However, Zhang Xun brilliantly defended the fort with his 3,000 men. The Yan suffered a devastating defeat, losing over 10,000 soldiers, and Linghu was forced to retreat.

But Linghu wasn't about to give up that easily. He returned the next month with a massive army of 40,000 men. Zhang Xun only had 2,000 able-bodied Tang soldiers remaining.

Instead of waiting in the fortress for help to come as Linghu expected, Zhang led 1,000 soldiers to charge at the Yang army, inflicting massive damage. THe Yan retreated for several miles to regroup, which allowed Zhang's men to return to the safety of the Yongqiu.

Linghu next tried a direct attack using seige ladders. Predicting this move, Zhang had ordered his men to make dozens of grass balls soaked in oil. He dropped these on the troops ascending the ladders, causing many enemy soldiers to burn or fall to their deaths.

The rebel army was low on morale after two humiliating defeats, so Linghu decided to just wait it out. He surrounded Yongqiu, cutting off all means of escape and waiting for the Tang troops to starve.
